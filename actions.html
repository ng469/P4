Actions and Solutions

What can be done to decrease the impact of climate change?

Whether we can halt global warming long enough to avert its worst effects depends on what we do between now and 2030. We must significantly reduce emissions and take some carbon dioxide out of the atmosphere. Fortunately, plants naturally take up and store carbon. We can store billions of tons of this "living carbon" by preserving natural habitats and judiciously managing cropland and forests. Here are some of the ways climate change can be controlled.

All key sources shifting to renewable energy sources:

With an emphasis on activities that may be implemented by the energy, industry, agricultural, transportation, nature-based solutions, and urban planning sectors, the United Nations identified a six-sector solution to combat climate change. The UN Environment Programme predicts that if all of these steps are taken, we could cut global carbon emissions by 29 to 32 gigatonnes, keeping the rise in world temperature to 1.5 °C.This strategy includes switching to renewable energy sources at home and at work, which is a vital component. According to the UNEP, we have the technology to reduce our own energy usage by switching to renewable energy sources and using less energy.

Finance low-carbon options in urban planning:

Governments must make comparable commitments with our expanding cities in addition to low-carbon choices for public transit. Whether they are flats or entertainment venues, new buildings present a chance to recognize green design strategies that lessen the burden on urban resources. (Fun fact: During the bright and sunny summer months, the Stavros Niarchos Cultural Center in Athens runs nearly entirely off of solar panels.) Rolling blackouts and brownouts, especially during the summer, have shown the damage that excessive power use can cause in areas like New York. Public infrastructure improvements that lessen our dependence on the electricity grid will help prevent the system from reaching unsustainable levels of overload.

Use public transportation, electric vehicles, and other non-motorized options to get around:

If you aren't seriously considering buying a new automobile, the carbon savings of trading in your current vehicle for an electric one are essentially lost. However, the key to reducing the greenhouse gas emissions from fuel-based motor vehicles is the widespread usage of electric vehicles and public transportation, as well as walking, bicycling, skating, and scootering. You can also bring up this matter with your elected leaders. For instance, you might recall reading that President Biden had urged the US Postal Service to include electric vans in its new fleet earlier this year. This didn't happen, but improvements like these — changes that go beyond any one person's mode of transportation — are what are required.

Invest in Disaster Risk Reduction(DRR):

DRR, or disaster risk reduction, safeguards the lives and livelihoods of groups of people and communities who are most at risk from emergencies or disasters. DRR lessens the negative effects on those who stand to lose the most, regardless of whether the crisis is brought on by nature, people, or a combination of both. While we can't now remove much of the effects of climate change, we can assist the communities who are most negatively impacted in preparing for and responding to calamities when they occur.

Advocate to fair financing and climate justice:

DRR tactics as well as other resilience, adaptation, and mitigation techniques are, of course, expensive. That which the nations most impacted by climate change frequently lack. Countries with the greatest carbon footprints should compensate those with lower footprints, who are typically more sensitive to global warming, as part of a worldwide commitment to climate justice. Increased funding is required for DRR and disaster prevention techniques like early warning and response systems, forecast-based financing structures, and adaptive infrastructure in nations like the United States. Additionally, these monies must be made swiftly dispersible and adaptable so that they can be immediately accessed in case of emergency.
